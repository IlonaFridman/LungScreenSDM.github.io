Public Health Impact

Lung cancer is the most commonly diagnosed cancer in the world, with nearly 2.5 million new cases in 2022, accounting for 12.4% of all cancer diagnoses. It is also the leading cause of cancer-related death, responsible for 18% of global cancer mortality.

In the United States, lung cancer kills more people than breast, prostate, and colorectal cancers combined.

Early detection through lung cancer screening can reduce mortality by about 20%, yet screening uptake remains low. Fewer than 20% of eligible individuals currently participate.

One major reason is that shared decision making — conversations between patients and providers about the benefits and risks of screening — rarely happens in practice due to limited time, access to providers, and lack of awareness.

What We Are Doing

We are exploring the use of an AI-powered conversational agent to support shared decision-making for lung cancer screening.

This tool aims to help patients understand screening in clear, everyday language and feel prepared to make informed choices.

Key Research Questions

1
How can the conversation feel natural and trustworthy?
2
What makes the tool acceptable for people who may have never heard of lung cancer screening?
3
When and how should it be delivered to best support patients?

Why This Matters

When shared decision-making occurs, lung cancer screening uptake can increase by up to 20%.

Improving these conversations has the potential to detect cancer earlier, start treatment sooner, and ultimately save lives by closing a critical gap in lung cancer prevention.
